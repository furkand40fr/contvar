\title{ContVAR: Contrastive Graph Representation Learning for Protein Variant Function Prediction}
\author{Furkan Dursun \and Boran \c{C}ak{\i}l \and Enes Karaarslan \and Caner Ayvaz}
\date{}

\begin{abstract}
Determining the functional consequences of single amino acid variants (SAVs) requires more than assigning a single deleteriousness score: in many biological settings, the central question is which Gene Ontology (GO) functions are lost, gained, or preserved. We present ContVAR, a two-stage pipeline for term-resolved variant function prediction. ContVAR first trains a residual GATv2 encoder on hybrid residue graphs with ESM-2 node features using GO semantic triplets, and then specializes the encoder with DMS protein-family triplets through joint global and mutation-site contrastive losses. Frozen protein embeddings are passed to per-aspect feed-forward decoders for wild-type GO annotation and per-term LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL variant calls. Across encoder evaluations, GO semantic pretraining produces stable held-out retrieval performance, whereas phase~1 variant specialization is limited by the scarcity of variant-level training data and remains difficult under strict family-level generalization. In the decoder stage, wild-type GO annotation remains strongest when ESM-2 sequence embeddings are used alone or combined with graph embeddings. We further evaluate the complete variant interpretation setting on a curated GO function-change cohort containing 6,117 proteins, 17,359 variants, and 18,126 variant--GO matches. In this cohort, wild-type GO recovery is strong, but signed GOF/LOF prediction remains difficult under severe class imbalance, with balanced metrics such as MCC close to zero across most settings. These results show that ContVAR provides a reproducible framework for linking GO-aware graph representation learning with variant-level function-change assessment, while also indicating that robust signed GOF/LOF prediction will require larger and more diverse variant-effect datasets for phase~1 variant specialization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The functional annotation of proteins and the accurate interpretation of single amino acid variants (SAVs) are essential for unraveling disease mechanisms, understanding oncogenic transformations, and guiding targeted therapeutics. Advances in high-throughput sequencing have produced large catalogs of coding variation; however, experimental characterization remains costly and time-consuming, leaving many variants classified as variants of uncertain significance (VUS). For disease biology and clinical interpretation, it is not sufficient to know only whether a coding mutation is likely to be harmful; it is also necessary to identify which molecular function, biological process, or cellular role is lost or newly gained.

Computational variant effect prediction (VEP) methods are therefore widely used. Classical approaches, such as SIFT~\cite{ng2001predicting}, PolyPhen-2~\cite{adzhubei2010method}, and CADD~\cite{kircher2014general}, rely on conservation and hand-crafted descriptors to estimate deleteriousness. More recent sequence-first models, including language-model-based VEP paradigms~\cite{meier2021language} and large-scale protein language models~\cite{lin2023evolutionary}, improve zero/few-shot generalization by learning distributional constraints from large corpora. However, most VEP pipelines still optimize scalar pathogenicity, fitness, or damagingness signals rather than structured function-level objectives. As a result, they can indicate that a variant is disruptive but usually cannot report the specific Gene Ontology (GO) function that is lost, the new function that is gained, or the strength of that change.

In parallel, automated protein function prediction has moved from sequence-similarity transfer to deep neural annotation models such as DeepGOPlus~\cite{kulmanov2019deepgoplus}, DeepFRI~\cite{gligorijevic2021structure}, TALE~\cite{cao2021tale}, and network-aware graph models such as DeepGraphGO~\cite{zhang2021deepgraphgo}. These methods are effective for wild-type GO assignment, but they are generally not designed to compare a wild-type protein with its SAV-bearing variants. Consequently, they do not directly address the central variant-interpretation problem: automatically predicting whether an existing function is lost, whether a new function is gained, and how strongly the mutation changes each affected function.

\paragraph{Problem statement and objective.}
The problem addressed in this work is the lack of a computational system that predicts the specific functional effects of SAVs at the level of gained and lost protein functions. Existing approaches largely stop at the presence or absence of a deleterious effect, whereas the experimentally relevant question is which molecular function or biological process changes. Determining this information experimentally requires carefully planned bioinformatics analyses, structural biology, and functional assays, making it impractical to apply at the scale of the hundreds of thousands of variants stored in public databases.

This limitation is especially important for two application areas. First, in genetic disease interpretation, function-resolved variant effects can connect mutations to biological mechanism rather than only to a pathogenicity label. Second, in protein engineering, large synthetic variant libraries require computational screening methods that can identify variants with desired functional properties before expensive experimental testing. The difficulty is amplified by the lack of experimentally solved three-dimensional structures for most variants and by the absence of a large supervised dataset in which SAVs are annotated with explicit GO-level loss- and gain-of-function labels.

The objective of ContVAR is therefore to move beyond binary deleteriousness prediction and estimate term-resolved functional change for SAVs. The guiding research question is whether advanced representation-learning methods can predict, with useful accuracy, (i) which molecular functions and biological roles change after a SAV, (ii) whether each affected function is lost or gained, and (iii) the severity of this change on a spectrum from no detectable effect to complete loss or gain of function.

This work introduces ContVAR, a graph metric-learning framework that explicitly connects GO-structured semantic priors with DMS-driven contrastive supervision. ContVAR builds residue graphs from monomer coordinates (e.g., AlphaFold2 models from the AlphaFold Database~\cite{jumper2021highly,varadi2022afdb}), combines ESM-2 embeddings with residue identity encodings, and trains a shared encoder with ontology-specific phase~0 heads followed by phase~1 DMS specialization under a joint global+local objective. The frozen encoder is paired with per-aspect GO decoders so that wild-type and variant embeddings can be compared as per-term LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL calls rather than collapsed into a single pathogenicity score.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Background concepts}
Metric learning on biological objects often uses triplet-style objectives to impose relative distances under weak labels. In protein representation learning, this strategy is attractive when categorical labels are incomplete but pairwise or ranking signals are available (e.g., mutational fitness ordering, semantic similarity). This is relevant to SAV function-change prediction because direct supervised labels for specific GO-level losses and gains are sparse, while weaker signals are available from GO semantic similarity, mutational fitness assays, and curated variant descriptions. Graph neural networks additionally permit explicit spatial topology and residue-level context, which are difficult to capture with sequence-only encoders.

\subsection{Recent approaches}

\paragraph{Variant effect prediction.}
Early VEP tools rely on evolutionary conservation and biophysical heuristics. SIFT~\cite{ng2001predicting} classifies substitutions as tolerated or damaging by comparing amino acid properties at conserved positions derived from homologous sequences. PolyPhen-2~\cite{adzhubei2010method} combines sequence-based features with structural information (solvent accessibility, dihedral angles) in a Naive Bayes classifier. CADD~\cite{kircher2014general} moves beyond single-residue heuristics by integrating dozens of heterogeneous annotations (conservation, regulatory context, protein features) into a single pathogenicity score via a trained support vector machine.

The advent of large protein language models opened a sequence-first paradigm for VEP. ESM-1v~\cite{meier2021language} performs zero-shot prediction of the effect of missense mutations by scoring sequences with a masked-language-model objective, demonstrating that evolutionary co-variation implicit in pre-training data captures functional constraints without explicit labels. ESM-2~\cite{lin2023evolutionary} scales this to 650M--15B parameters and achieves strong performance on DMS benchmarks such as ProteinGym~\cite{notin2023proteingym}. AlphaMissense~\cite{cheng2023alphamissense} further demonstrates the clinical relevance of learned missense-variant scoring at proteome scale by producing pathogenicity-oriented predictions for human missense substitutions. These methods are valuable for ranking variant impact, but they are not designed to return term-level functional changes.

\paragraph{Protein function prediction.}
DeepGOPlus~\cite{kulmanov2019deepgoplus} combines per-residue CNN features over sequences with a sequence-similarity transfer step, reporting mAP gains over sequence-similarity alone across all three GO aspects. TALE~\cite{cao2021tale} uses a Transformer with a joint sequence-label embedding that ties GO term representation to sequence features, improving annotation on sparsely annotated functions. DeepFRI~\cite{gligorijevic2021structure} constructs residue contact graphs from PDB structures and applies graph convolutional networks, showing that structure-derived topology captures functional relationships missed by sequence alone. DeepGraphGO~\cite{zhang2021deepgraphgo} aggregates protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks with sequence features through a GNN, demonstrating large-scale multispecies GO prediction. These systems address protein-level function annotation, not mutation-resolved functional change. A high-quality wild-type GO predictor can identify a protein's functional background, but it does not by itself determine which annotation is removed, strengthened, newly introduced, or preserved after a specific amino acid substitution.

Despite this progress, the two families address adjacent but not identical problems: VEP models output variant-level disruption scores, while GO annotation models assign terms to wild-type sequences. Neither family explicitly enforces mutation-sensitive metric geometry that respects GO-level functional differences or provides an automatic mechanism for term-resolved gain/loss calling.

\subsection{Comparative discussion}
The reviewed methods differ mainly in the biological signal they model and in the form of prediction they return. Sequence-first VEP models are strong at ranking missense substitutions because large protein language models capture evolutionary and distributional constraints, but their outputs are usually scalar and do not identify the affected GO term. Structure-aware GO predictors add residue-contact information and can recover wild-type functional annotations, yet they are not formulated as wild-type/variant comparison systems. Direct multi-label GO classifiers provide interpretable term probabilities, but they require dense supervised annotations and do not by themselves define a mutation-sensitive representation space. Metric-learning objectives are useful when complete term-level gain/loss supervision is unavailable, because they can impose relative similarity constraints from weaker signals such as GO semantic relatedness or DMS fitness orderings.

ContVAR is positioned at the intersection of these families. It uses sequence-derived ESM-2 features and residue-graph topology to represent proteins, but trains the encoder with contrastive objectives rather than only direct GO classification. Phase~0 emphasizes GO-semantic organization of the latent space, while phase~1 tests whether DMS-derived mutation triplets and mutation-site supervision can make the representation more sensitive to variant effects. The downstream decoder then translates these embeddings back into GO-term probabilities for wild-type and variant comparison.

\subsection{Research gap}
The remaining gap is therefore not the absence of sequence models, structure-based function predictors, or GO classifiers individually, but the absence of a unified variant-function pipeline that connects them into one term-resolved inference task. Specifically, prior pipelines rarely combine (a) GO-semantic constraints during representation learning, (b) mutation-level triplet supervision, (c) both whole-graph and mutation-site contrastive objectives with memory-aware hard-negative mining~\cite{schroff2015facenet}, and (d) interpretable per-term LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL outputs. This gap motivates the ContVAR design and aligns the method with the central research question: whether SAV-induced changes in molecular function and biological role can be predicted automatically, including the direction and severity of the functional change.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

ContVAR is organized as two training stages that share a common protein identifier space:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mutation-aware graph encoder.} Training proceeds in two phases. \textbf{Phase~0} performs GO semantic-similarity triplet pretraining with ontology-specific heads (MF, BP, CC) on residue graphs. \textbf{Phase~1} specializes the same backbone on DMS protein-family triplets with a joint global graph-level and local mutation-site contrastive loss; streaming semi-hard negative mining is active from the first phase~1 epoch.
    \item \textbf{Supervised GO decoder and variant function-change inference.} A feed-forward decoder (\texttt{FFNDecoder}) is trained on frozen encoder embeddings as a multi-label GO classifier under UniRef50 cluster-aware splits~\cite{suzek2015uniref}, separately for each GO aspect (MF, BP, CC). At inference, variant embeddings are scored and combined with curated wild-type (WT) GO annotations from GOA to produce per-GO LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL calls.
\end{enumerate}
Encoder and decoder use different supervision signals---DMS-driven contrastive geometry versus protein-level GO multi-label annotations---and are linked through a persisted encoder checkpoint and protein embeddings keyed by UniProt identifier.

\subsection{Dataset Description}
\label{sec:dataset-description}
\textbf{GO (phase~0) track.} Phase~0 consumes residue graphs and ontology-specific semantic-similarity tables under a UniRef50-aware protein split~\cite{suzek2015uniref}, which contains 122,190 proteins (97,771 train, 12,093 validation, 12,326 test). Positives use similarity $\geq 0.8$; negatives use similarity $\leq 0.2$ and are sampled from low/mid bins ([0.0,0.1), [0.1,0.2]) for hardness balancing. 

\textbf{DMS (phase~1) track.} The mutagenesis corpus is organized by protein family with one wild-type anchor, multiple positives, and multiple negatives per family. Starting from the ProteinGym Deep Mutational Scanning benchmark~\cite{notin2023proteingym}, we retained proteins with sequence length at most 656 residues, giving 97 unique proteins. When an assay annotated only a protein segment, the corresponding full-length sequence was reconstructed and variant coordinates were remapped to the full-length residue index so that the graph encoder always received complete protein context. For each protein, the positive pool was built from 50 neutral variants selected immediately above the assay cutoff indicating retained-functionality while the negative pool used the 50 lowest-fitness score variants as deleterious examples; selection was sequence-position stratified to reduce clustering in a single domain or local region. Thus, the DMS dataset contains 9,700 curated variants in total ($97$ proteins $\times$ $100$ variants per protein) before graph construction. The DMS split respects the UniRef50-aware protein split used for phase~0 and decoder training: if a DMS protein is present in that split, the wild type and all of its variants inherit the same train/validation/test assignment; DMS proteins absent from the UniRef50 split are conservatively assigned to training. The resulting fixed DMS split contains 97 families (93 train, 2 validation, 2 test), preventing variant-level leakage across held-out protein families. Raw structures are consumed as mmCIF/CIF files, and per-residue ESM-2 embeddings are loaded from a precomputed store keyed by normalized protein identifiers. Mutated residue positions are parsed from variant filenames during collation and mining.

\textbf{GO annotation / decoder track.} The supervised decoder uses the same 122,190-protein dataset and the same UniRef50-aware train/validation/test split as phase~0 (97,771 train, 12,093 validation, 12,326 test), but reformulates the data as protein-level multi-label GO prediction rather than semantic triplet learning. Curated UniProt-GOA Swiss-Prot annotations~\cite{uniprot2023,geneontology2021} provide Molecular Function (MF), Biological Process (BP), and Cellular Component (CC) labels after excluding electronic (\texttt{IEA}) and ``no data'' (\texttt{ND}) evidence codes. GO terms annotated in at least ten proteins are retained, yielding 3{,}258 MF terms, 8{,}336 BP terms, and 1{,}577 CC terms before appending a dedicated \texttt{NULL\_FUNCTION} output.

\textbf{GO function-change cohort.} Because no directly usable dataset links protein variants to GO-level loss- or gain-of-function effects, we constructed a curated cohort from UniProt Swiss-Prot \texttt{variant} and \texttt{mutagenesis} feature descriptions. The cohort converts free-text experimental descriptions into atomic variant--GO effect records with a signed effect label (LOSS or GAIN). After description filtering, GO matching, manual rule-based cleanup, and project-specific sequence-length filtering, the final cohort used in ContVAR contains 6,117 proteins; 17,359 variants; 18,126 variant--GO term matches.

\textbf{Structure interchange vs.\ cached graphs.} CIF/mmCIF is the portable format for atomic coordinates; serialized graph tensors cache parsing, featurization, and edge construction for reuse.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation / Preprocessing}
\textbf{Structure sources.} For the selected DMS wild-type and variant sequences, three-dimensional structures were generated with AlphaFold3~\cite{abramson2024alphafold3}. To reduce folding cost on the compute cluster, available assay/MSA resources were reused instead of regenerating alignments from scratch. For the larger GO protein set used in phase~0 and decoder embedding export, residue graphs were built from already available structures, primarily AlphaFold Database (AFDB) AlphaFold2 models~\cite{varadi2022afdb,jumper2021highly} when present and alternative structure resources otherwise. All structures are treated as fixed mmCIF/CIF inputs for graph construction. Because phase~0 and the decoder consume AFDB-derived graphs while phase~1 DMS training uses project AF3 predictions, we characterized DMS wild-type concordance between the two structure sources before training (Section~\ref{sec:wt-structure-concordance}).

\textbf{From structures to residue graphs.} Per-residue $\mathrm{C}_\alpha$ coordinates and amino-acid identities are extracted from monomer structures and assembled into PyTorch Geometric graphs~\cite{fey2019pyg}. Connectivity uses the hybrid edge scheme below; an optional pure $k$NN baseline (Graphein-style~\cite{ramirez2022graphein}) is available for ablations.

Node features concatenate a residue-identity one-hot and a per-residue ESM-2 embedding:
\[
\mathbf{x}_i = [\text{AAOneHot}_i \, \| \, \text{ESM2}_i] \in \mathbb{R}^{20+1280} = \mathbb{R}^{1300}.
\]
ESM-2 (650M-parameter variant~\cite{lin2023evolutionary}) is chosen as the per-residue feature source because it produces high-quality sequence-contextualized representations without requiring multiple sequence alignment (MSA) construction at inference time. The embeddings are pre-computed and frozen throughout encoder training, so the GATv2 encoder learns mutation-sensitive spatial geometry on top of fixed evolutionary-chemical context rather than attempting to learn both from scratch.

\textbf{GOA annotation file preparation.} Gene Ontology annotations were obtained from the UniProt-GOA GAF distribution ~\cite{uniprot2023,geneontology2021}. Three filters were applied to the raw download to produce \texttt{goa\_2025-12-04\_swissprot\_noiea.tsv}: (i) annotations supported only by the electronic (\texttt{IEA}) evidence code were removed; (ii) only entries with a UniProtKB/Swiss-Prot (manually reviewed) accession were retained; and (iii) isoform-specific accessions (identifiers containing a hyphen, e.g.\ \texttt{P12345-2}) were filtered out, since isoform entries are almost never reviewed and their GO annotations are sparse and predominantly automatic. The resulting file contains 2{,}211{,}880 annotation rows, comprising 1{,}277{,}471 unique protein--GO term pairs over 166{,}613 Swiss-Prot proteins and 27{,}053 distinct GO terms across all three aspects; this protein and term count is further narrowed by the embedding/split intersection and the per-aspect minimum-frequency filter described below and in Section~\ref{sec:dataset-description}. Table~\ref{tab:goa-evidence-codes} reports the evidence-code composition of these rows; by ontology aspect, 36.6\% (809{,}656) are Molecular Function, 34.8\% (770{,}613) Biological Process, and 28.6\% (631{,}611) Cellular Component annotations. The \texttt{ND} (``no biological data'', 1.2\%) rows remaining in this file are additionally excluded during decoder label construction. For reference, the unfiltered UniProt-GOA distribution (all UniProtKB entries, all organisms, all evidence codes including \texttt{IEA}) contains 962{,}163{,}577 annotation rows; the filtered subset above retains $\approx$0.23\% of this total, the remainder being dominated by electronic annotations on unreviewed (TrEMBL) entries across many organisms.

\textbf{Decoder label preparation.} After encoder training, protein-level graph embeddings are exported to H5 and matched to GOA annotations by UniProt identifier. For each ontology, annotations are converted into multi-hot vectors over GO terms observed in at least ten proteins; a \texttt{NULL\_FUNCTION} output is appended to the vocabulary. Proteins without both an exported embedding and GOA annotation for the selected aspect are excluded from decoder training and evaluation.

\textbf{GO function-change cohort construction.} We first downloaded all Swiss-Prot \texttt{variant} and \texttt{mutagenesis} feature records. Since a single feature description can describe multiple functional consequences, descriptions were split into atomic statements. Descriptions matching deterministic syntactic patterns were split with rule-based rules; ambiguous multi-effect descriptions were split with an LLM. Statements describing only weak or uncertain effects were removed: descriptions containing qualitative modifiers such as ``slight'' or ``moderate'', or quantitative increase/decrease estimates below 50\%, were excluded. This filtering step retained 129,989 atomic descriptions.

Each atomic description was then assigned an effect category using rule-based lexical patterns. Loss-of-function descriptions were identified from terms such as ``abolish'' and ``impair'', gain-of-function descriptions from terms such as ``gain'' and ``enhance'', and location descriptions from cellular localization phrases such as ``nuclear entry'' or ``cytoplasmic redistribution''. Location was separated at this stage because GO matching used only MF and BP terms for ordinary loss/gain statements, but only CC terms for localization statements. Descriptions that represented effects not expressible as GO terms, such as generic protein stability changes, were discarded.

For GO term assignment, each remaining atomic description and all candidate GO terms were embedded with SciBERT~\cite{beltagy2019scibert}. Matching was performed in two passes. First, each variant description was matched against the wild-type protein's existing GO annotations, using the highest cosine-similarity GO term when the similarity was at least 0.87. Second, descriptions without an acceptable wild-type annotation match were searched against the full GO term pool with a stricter minimum similarity of 0.90. Although this full-pool fallback was initially intended for gain-of-function descriptions, it was also applied to loss-of-function descriptions because some experimentally described lost functions were absent from the wild-type annotation set. Descriptions still unmatched after embedding search were passed to an LLM together with the ten nearest GO terms by embedding similarity; the LLM selected a term only when one of the candidates expressed the described function, otherwise the record was discarded.

Localization records were finally converted into signed LOSS or GAIN labels according to directionality. For example, increased nuclear localization was treated as gain of the corresponding localization term, whereas decreased localization to the nucleus was treated as loss. We also corrected systematic embedding errors in which amino-acid or nucleic-acid specificity dominated the match incorrectly. Cases such as an alanine-related description matching a serine-specific GO term, or tRNA-related descriptions matching rRNA-specific terms, were detected rule-wise and replaced by the appropriate parent GO term (e.g.\ RNA acetylation rather than a mismatched RNA-species-specific child). Lastly, a small set of overly general GO terms that produced many high-similarity false matches, such as enzyme inhibitor activity, was identified during manual inspection and all of their matches were removed, eliminating approximately 1,000 records. For compatibility with the adopted graph construction and downstream AlphaFold structure prediction, we retained only proteins with sequence length between 49 and 1,280 residues, yielding the final 6,117-protein, 17,359-variant, 18,126-match cohort.

\subsubsection{Hybrid residue-graph connectivity}
\label{sec:hybrid-graph}
The same hybrid residue-graph construction is used for protein graphs across the encoder workflow, including GO semantic pretraining and DMS specialization. The design is motivated by the need to represent both local structural neighborhoods and non-local residue coupling: in the DMS setting, for example, variants can perturb local side-chain packing as well as distant contacts. A purely sequence-local chain graph under-represents 3D contacts; a fixed-radius spatial graph can miss compensatory long-range interactions; and a dense residue--residue graph is prohibitive for long chains. ContVAR therefore uses a \emph{fixed neighbor budget per residue} that mixes three complementary link types.

Let $N$ be the number of residues in a chain, $\mathbf{r}_i\in\mathbb{R}^3$ the $\mathrm{C}_\alpha$ coordinate of residue $i$, and $s_i$ its sequence index. Pairwise Euclidean distances $d_{ij}=\|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j\|_2$ and index distances $|s_i-s_j|$ are computed within the same chain (cross-chain links are masked). For each residue $i$, neighbors are selected in three sequential stages; edges already chosen in an earlier stage are excluded from later stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sequence-index neighbors} ($k_{\text{idx}}$, default 16): the $k_{\text{idx}}$ residues $j\neq i$ on the same chain with smallest $|s_i-s_j|$.
    \item \textbf{Spatial neighbors} ($k_{\text{sp}}$, default 16): among remaining candidates, the $k_{\text{sp}}$ residues with smallest $d_{ij}$.
    \item \textbf{Stochastic long-range neighbors} ($k_{\text{rnd}}$, default 16): among remaining candidates, sample $k_{\text{rnd}}$ partners via a Gumbel top-$k$ trick~\cite{kool2019stochastic}. Unnormalized scores are $w_{ij}=-3\log(d_{ij}+\epsilon)$; independent Gumbel noise is added and the lowest perturbed scores are taken, biasing selection toward closer residues while retaining diversity (inverse-cube weighting in distance space).
\end{enumerate}
Directed edges $(i\to j)$ are created for every valid neighbor slot; invalid padding slots ($j=-1$) are dropped. Each edge carries a 20-dimensional attribute vector: (i) a 16-bin Gaussian radial basis expansion of $d_{ij}$ on $[0,d_{\max}]$ with $d_{\max}=22$\,\AA; (ii) a 3-way one-hot encoding of neighbor type (index / spatial / random); and (iii) one normalized sequence separation $|s_i-s_j|/N$. The GATv2 stack consumes these attributes through an edge MLP before attention (Section~\ref{sec:encoder-backbone}).

This hybrid residue-graph recipe is adapted from the SALAD sparse-neighbor construction, which combines chain-index, Euclidean nearest, and randomized long-range partners for structure modeling~\cite{jendrusch2025salad}. ContVAR uses the same connectivity principle for residue-level message passing on predicted monomer structures~\cite{jumper2021highly}, with $k{=}16$ per neighbor type by default and on-disk graph caching when structures are revisited.

\textbf{Mutation context.} The encoder always sees the full-chain graph; locality around the variant is enforced in the loss via the mutation-site branch rather than by cropping the graph to a fixed-radius neighborhood.

\subsubsection{Structure-source concordance (AF3 vs.\ AFDB AF2)}
\label{sec:wt-structure-concordance}
Phase~0 and decoder training rely primarily on AFDB monomer models (AF2), whereas phase~1 DMS triplets use in-house AF3 wild-type and variant predictions. Before committing to this split, we audited whether mixed sources would systematically change the \emph{fixed} residue graphs fed to the encoder, or whether disagreement is dominated by intrinsically flexible targets irrespective of the predictor. This is a dataset-quality check, not a model-evaluation result.

\paragraph{Global C$\alpha$ RMSD.}
Table~\ref{tab:wt-graph-summary} summarizes pairwise C$\alpha$ RMSD after superposition for $n{=}90$ DMS wild-types with in-house AF3, AFDB AF2, and ColabFold AF2 rank-1 structures~\cite{mirdita2022colabfold}. Seven families are excluded because no AFDB model was available (P03466, P03470, P03704, P04610, P04618, P16117, P03034). Three comparisons are reported: AFDB vs.\ project AF3 (phase~0 vs.\ phase~1 source), AFDB vs.\ ColabFold AF2, and project AF3 vs.\ ColabFold AF2. Cohort medians are \textbf{6.0}, \textbf{5.0}, and \textbf{6.1~\AA}, respectively (means 9.9, 9.5, and 10.1~\AA). Compact folds remain at the low end (e.g.\ TPK1 Q9H3S4, 0.17~\AA\ across all three pairs; GFP P42212, 3.0~\AA\ AFDB--AF3). The long tail is shared across comparisons (SPG1 P06654: 63.0, 65.0, and 57.8~\AA), indicating that elevated displacement on flexible targets is not specific to the AF2$\rightarrow$AF3 switch.

\paragraph{Cross-pipeline agreement at 7~\AA\ cutoff.}
To classify targets by structural stability across all three structure sources, we binarized each comparison at 7~\AA\ RMSD (Table~\ref{tab:wt-rmsd-categories}). \textbf{47} of 90 audited wild-types ($\sim$52\%) are low in all three pairwise comparisons; \textbf{34} are high in all three; only \textbf{9} are mixed (five with one high and two low; four with two high and one low). The \emph{all three low} set shows concordant, low-displacement folds with high deterministic graph overlap (Section~\ref{sec:wt-graph-overlap}), i.e.\ different predictors agree on both global coordinates and the fixed hybrid edge sets used in training. Most mixed-category proteins sit just above the 7~\AA\ threshold in one comparison while the other two remain low, so they behave like the stable cohort in practice. Only two identifiers---P63165 and P51681---show project AF3 clearly diverging from \emph{both} AFDB and ColabFold AF2; P51681 is also among the 26 targets with deterministic Jaccard $<0.8$. Proteins high across all three comparisons overlap strongly with the flexible long tail above (e.g.\ SPG1 P06654) and with low deterministic graph Jaccard, indicating that large displacements reflect intrinsic conformational variability rather than a systematic AF2$\rightarrow$AF3 pipeline effect.

\paragraph{Within-predictor variability (spot checks).}
Representative high-RMSD targets were additionally re-folded with AlphaFold Server (AF3) and ColabFold (custom MSA). On SPG1 (P06654), fresh AF3 and AFDB AF2 still differ by $\sim$56--66~\AA; within a single predictor, rank-1 vs.\ rank-2 models differ by $\sim$19~\AA\ (AlphaFold Server) and $\sim$33~\AA\ (ColabFold custom MSA). Low-RMSD proteins stay low across pipelines. ColabFold rank-1 vs.\ AFDB AF2 in the same audit therefore captures one AF2 reference point.

\paragraph{Residue-graph overlap.}
\label{sec:wt-graph-overlap}
Structures alone do not determine encoder inputs: graphs use the hybrid residue-graph connectivity above ($k{=}16$ per neighbor type). In a separate pass, we rebuilt graphs from AF3 and AFDB wild-type CIFs with the same code path as training (deterministic index/spatial neighbors; random neighbors with fixed seed 42) and compared edge sets by Jaccard overlap on residue-indexed pairs, together with Pearson correlation of per-residue degrees on shared indices ($n{=}90$, matching the RMSD audit cohort).

Across these 90 pairs, median Jaccard was \textbf{0.92} (all edges), \textbf{0.84} (spatial only), and \textbf{1.00} (sequence-index only); median degree-profile correlation was \textbf{0.96}. Global C$\alpha$ RMSD and graph overlap measure different aspects: many families show modest displacement in all three RMSD pairs while still sharing most hybrid residue-graph edges, whereas the flexible tail is high in both metrics.

Using deterministic overlap (index + spatial, excluding stochastic long-range links), 26 targets fell below 0.8 (Table~\ref{tab:wt-graph-summary}). On these 26 proteins, median AFDB--AF3 RMSD is \textbf{22.9~\AA}\ versus \textbf{2.8~\AA}\ for the other 64 ($n{=}90$); AFDB--ColabFold AF2 and AF3--ColabFold AF2 medians show the same split (18.9 and 20.9~\AA\ vs.\ 2.6 and 3.1~\AA). At the 7~\AA\ cutoff, 20 of 26 exceed the threshold in all three RMSD pairs, so low graph overlap and high structural displacement largely co-occur on the same flexible wild-types (e.g.\ YAP1, p53, CD19, SERT, $\alpha$-synuclein). Five audit-stable examples (P69222, P31969, O15245, P80324, P61991) remain below 7~\AA\ in all three comparisons (3.8--6.8~\AA\ AFDB--AF3) yet still show deterministic Jaccard $0.57$--$0.75$---modest graph agreement despite concordant global folds. For the remaining 64 families with deterministic Jaccard $\geq 0.8$, AF2/AF3 graph inputs are closely aligned.

\paragraph{Implications for the AFDB/AF3 split.}
The audit supports using AFDB AlphaFold2 graphs for phase~0 GO pretraining and project AF3 structures for phase~1 DMS triplets without harmonizing every target to a single predictor. The GO pretraining corpus is dominated by well-defined, globular proteins that fall into the concordant \emph{all three low} regime; for this majority, AFDB coordinates and hybrid residue graphs are stable across AF2 and AF3 sources, so phase~0 is unlikely to inherit a strong structure-predictor bias. On the flexible \emph{all three high} tail, large RMSD values appear consistently across AFDB, ColabFold, and in-house AF3, so disagreement is better explained by target-intrinsic disorder or multi-state behavior than by the choice to mix predictors across training phases. The nine mixed-category proteins do not define a separate failure mode for the pipeline: aside from P63165 and P51681, they are threshold-borderline cases aligned with the stable cohort. We therefore retain the phase~0 (AFDB) / phase~1 (AF3) structure split and the fixed hybrid edge construction described in Section~\ref{sec:hybrid-graph}.

\subsection{Model/Architecture Design}

\subsubsection{Mutation-Aware Graph Encoder (backbone)}
\label{sec:encoder-backbone}
The encoder is a residual two-layer GATv2 stack~\cite{brody2022how} with four attention heads per layer and hidden size 128. GATv2 is selected over standard GAT~\cite{velickovic2018gat} because the original GAT computes attention coefficients as a function of a linear combination of the concatenated source and target features, which Brody et al.~\cite{brody2022how} show collapses to a ranking over neighbors that is independent of the query node—making attention static. GATv2 remedies this by applying the non-linearity \emph{before} the attention projection so that each coefficient depends jointly on both the query and the key, yielding fully dynamic attention. In the context of irregular protein residue graphs, where the chemical environment around a mutation site varies substantially across different protein families and positions, this dynamic re-weighting is important: the encoder must learn to attend to non-local compensatory contacts whose relevance changes depending on which residue is mutated. Edge attributes are first projected by an MLP and injected into both GATv2 layers. Let $\mathbf{h}_i^{(l)}$ be node state at layer $l$; each layer applies attention-weighted aggregation with edge conditioning, layer normalization, and residual addition. Global embedding uses mean pooling followed by a projection head to 256 dimensions and $\ell_2$ normalization. A separate local projection head extracts the embedding at mutation position; if a residue index is absent, graph mean is used as fallback.

\subsubsection{Phase 0: GO semantic pretraining}
Phase~0 provides biologically informed initialization by training ontology-specific triplets (MF/BP/CC) using ontology heads on top of the shared encoder. For each ontology $o$:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GO},o}=\max(0,\ d(\mathbf{z}_a^o,\mathbf{z}_p^o)-d(\mathbf{z}_a^o,\mathbf{z}_n^o)+m_{go}),
\]
with default $m_{go}=0.2$. Active ontology losses are averaged per step. Optional ratio-based ontology sampling (default MF:BP:CC = 0.5:0.3:0.2) prevents dominance by a single ontology.

\subsubsection{Phase 1: DMS encoder training (global triplet + local contrastive loss)}
\label{sec:phase1-dms}
After phase~0, phase~1 loads DMS protein-family triplets under the UniRef50-respecting fixed split described above (97 families: 93 train, 2 validation, 2 test) and initializes weights from the phase~0 checkpoint. Phase~1 uses streaming semi-hard negative mining~\cite{schroff2015facenet} from the first training epoch: each epoch reshuffles protein families, samples positives, scans candidate negatives in chunks, and rebuilds mined batches online. Exhaustive positive$\times$negative enumeration is reserved for validation and test.

\paragraph{Per-batch mining and forward pass.}
Families are shuffled and grouped into mining batches. Gradients are accumulated over four mined batches before each optimizer update. For each batch:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item One positive variant per family is sampled; anchor and positive residue graphs are loaded from cache or built on the fly.
    \item The current model embeds anchors and positives (no gradient) to obtain $d_{ap}=d(\mathbf{z}_a^g,\mathbf{z}_p^g)$.
    \item All candidate negatives listed for that family are scanned in chunks of 10 structures; negatives that are hard ($d_{an}<d_{ap}$) or semi-hard ($d_{ap}\le d_{an}<d_{ap}+m$) are retained and up to 10 per family are subsampled at random; if none qualify, the closest negative is used.
    \item A gradient step runs on the mined set; the global triplet hinge $\max(0, d_{ap}-d_{an}+m)$ is averaged over all qualifying negatives in the batch.
\end{enumerate}
The global loss is therefore always computed on mined hard/semi-hard negatives, not on a fixed random subset chosen once per epoch.

\paragraph{Losses.}
For mined negatives, the global branch is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{g}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{n\in\mathcal{Q}}\max\bigl(0,\ d(\mathbf{z}_a^g,\mathbf{z}_p^g)-d(\mathbf{z}_a^g,\mathbf{z}_n^g)+m\bigr),
\]
where $\mathcal{Q}$ is the set of hard and semi-hard negatives retained for the batch. The local branch supervises mutation-site embeddings. Global mean pooling can dilute mutation-specific signal in long proteins; the local head extracts the embedding at the parsed mutation index and applies a pairwise contrastive loss that attracts anchor--positive pairs and repels anchor--negative pairs at the mutation site:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{l}=\frac{1}{2B}\sum_{i=1}^{2B}\left[y_i d_i^2 + (1-y_i)\max(0,m-d_i)^2\right],
\]
with $y_i=1$ on positive pairs and $y_i=0$ on negative pairs. The hardest global negative per family (index from mining stats) is reused to select which mutation-site negative enters $\mathcal{L}_l$. The step objective is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}=\frac{\mathcal{L}_{g}+\mathcal{L}_{l}}{2}.
\]

\paragraph{Optimization, validation, and checkpoints.}
Phase~1 training runs for 200 epochs by default. Optimizer: AdamW (learning rate $10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01) with gradient accumulation over four steps and gradient clipping at norm 1.0. Validation and test enumerate every positive$\times$negative pair per family for stable ranking metrics. The phase~1 checkpoint with lowest validation $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$ is retained for embedding export and decoder training.

\subsubsection{Supervised GO decoder}
\label{sec:decoder}

\paragraph{Scope.}
The decoder consumes protein-level embeddings from the trained encoder (256 dimensions) or, in ablations, raw ESM-2 embeddings (1{,}280 dimensions) or their concatenation (1{,}536 dimensions). It outputs multi-label GO probabilities under Molecular Function (MF), Biological Process (BP), and Cellular Component (CC). One decoder is trained independently per aspect.

\paragraph{Output vocabulary.}
The decoder output dimensionality is set by the aspect-specific GO vocabularies prepared in the GO annotation / decoder track described above: 3{,}258 Molecular Function (MF) terms, 8{,}336 Biological Process (BP) terms, and 1{,}577 Cellular Component (CC) terms, plus a dedicated \texttt{NULL\_FUNCTION} class for each aspect to flag predicted complete loss of function.

\paragraph{Training labels and splits.}
Decoder training uses the GO annotation / decoder track described in Section~\ref{sec:dataset-description}. Each protein is represented by a multi-hot label vector over the relevant aspect vocabulary, and all three decoder aspects use the same UniRef50-aware protein split as phase~0.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
\texttt{FFNDecoder} is a feed-forward network. Each hidden block applies batch normalization, ReLU activation, and dropout (tapered by a factor of 0.67 in the last hidden block). The head outputs logits per GO term; sigmoid is applied at inference. Architecture hyperparameters---hidden widths, dropout rate, weight decay, and positive-weight clamp---are selected by a three-stage sequential grid search (described below), independently for each aspect$\times$embedding-type pair.

\paragraph{Class-balanced multi-label loss.}
Training uses binary cross-entropy with logits and positive-class weights. For GO term $c$,
\[
w_c \;=\; \min\!\left(\frac{N - n_c^{+}}{n_c^{+}+\varepsilon},\ \tau\right),\qquad
w_{\text{NULL\_FUNCTION}} \;\leftarrow\; \alpha \cdot w_{\text{NULL\_FUNCTION}},
\]
where $n_c^{+}$ is the number of training proteins with term $c$, $N$ is the training set size, and $\alpha=3.0$. The clamp $\tau$ is tuned in stage~3 of the grid search.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter selection.}
Decoder hyperparameters are tuned via a three-stage sequential grid search, run independently per aspect and embedding type, with each stage selecting the configuration maximizing validation mAP:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stage~1} (learning rate $\times$ dropout): $\text{lr}\in\{2.5\!\times\!10^{-4},\,5\!\times\!10^{-4},\,10^{-3}\}$, $\text{dropout}\in\{0.2,\,0.3,\,0.4\}$ (9 runs).
    \item \textbf{Stage~2} (architecture $\times$ weight decay): hidden widths $\in\{[512,512],\,[1024,512],\,[512,1024,512]\}$, weight decay $\in\{10^{-5},\,10^{-4},\,5\!\times\!10^{-4}\}$ (9 runs, seeded from stage~1 winner).
    \item \textbf{Stage~3} (positive-weight clamp $\tau$): $\tau\in\{10,\,20,\,40\}$ (3 runs, seeded from stage~2 winner).
\end{enumerate}
Stages~1--3 each train for 30 epochs with patience 6. The winning configuration is then retrained in a final run for up to 100 epochs with patience 20. Across all evaluated conditions the grid search consistently selected $\text{lr}=10^{-3}$, $\text{dropout}=0.2$, and hidden widths $1024\!\to\!512$; weight decay and $\tau$ varied by embedding type (e.g.\ $\text{wd}=10^{-5}$, $\tau=10$ for ContVAR-only; $\text{wd}=10^{-4}$, $\tau=10$ for ESM-only).

\paragraph{Optimization and schedule.}
AdamW optimizer; cosine annealing over the epoch budget; batch size 256. The best validation mAP checkpoint is kept; training stops if mAP fails to improve by more than $10^{-4}$ for twenty consecutive epochs. Metrics are logged to Weights \& Biases.

\paragraph{Decoder evaluation metrics.}
Validation and test use logits converted to probabilities. GO terms with zero positives in a split are excluded from mAP. For thresholded metrics we sweep a probability grid from 0.05 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05 and select the threshold maximizing macro F1; at the selected threshold we report macro-averaged F1, precision, recall, subset accuracy, and MCC.

\paragraph{Variant function-change inference.}
The decoder is run on \emph{variant} embeddings only; wild-type GO annotations are looked up directly from GOA (the database is the source of truth for the WT). For a variant with embedding $\mathbf{z}_{\text{var}}$ let $\mathbf{s}=\sigma(\text{FFNDecoder}(\mathbf{z}_{\text{var}}))\in[0,1]^C$. For each annotated WT GO term $g$:
\[
\text{status}(g) =
\begin{cases}
\text{LOSS} & \text{if } g \in \mathrm{WT}_{\text{GO}} \text{ and } s_g < 1-\theta,\\
\text{GAIN} & \text{if } g \notin \mathrm{WT}_{\text{GO}} \text{ and } s_g > \theta,\\
\text{NEUTRAL} & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\]
with default $\theta=0.1$. Variant outputs are therefore \emph{term-resolved} function-change calls rather than a single pooled score over all wild-type annotations.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\paragraph{Encoder-side metrics.}
During phase~0 and phase~1 we log total, global, and local losses; global and local distance margins; streaming-mining statistics for hard, semi-hard, easy, evaluated, and qualifying candidate negatives; and three geometry/retrieval diagnostics on $\ell_2$-normalized global embeddings~\cite{wang2020hypersphere}. Validation and test use exhaustive positive$\times$negative combinations per family. The encoder checkpoint with lowest validation total loss is re-evaluated on held-out families before decoder training.

\textbf{Alignment} measures how close anchor and positive embeddings are. Following Wang and Isola~\cite{wang2020hypersphere}, we report the mean squared Euclidean distance between paired embeddings,
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\|\mathbf{z}_a^{(i)}-\mathbf{z}_p^{(i)}\|_2^2,
\]
where $B$ is the batch size. Lower values indicate that functionally similar variants (anchors and their positives) occupy nearby points on the unit hypersphere---the ``alignment'' objective of contrastive learning.

\textbf{Uniformity} quantifies how evenly embeddings fill the hypersphere rather than collapsing into a narrow cone. On the concatenated set of anchor, positive, and negative embeddings $\{\mathbf{z}\}$, we compute
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{unif}} \;=\; \log\!\left(\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{(\mathbf{z}_i,\mathbf{z}_j)\in\mathcal{P}} e^{-t\,\|\mathbf{z}_i-\mathbf{z}_j\|_2^2}\right),
\]
with temperature $t{=}2$ and $\mathcal{P}$ the set of unordered pairs ($|\mathcal{P}|$ from pairwise distances). Lower (more negative) values indicate greater spread. This matches the reference implementation (\url{https://github.com/ssnl/align_uniform}) of Wang and Isola~\cite{wang2020hypersphere}.

\textbf{Mean reciprocal rank (MRR)} evaluates retrieval within each mini-batch. For anchor $i$, all positives and negatives in the batch form a candidate pool; candidates are ranked by ascending $\ell_2$ distance to the anchor. The rank $r_i$ of the matching positive (the $i$-th positive) yields $\mathrm{MRR}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_i 1/r_i$. MRR${=}1$ when every anchor retrieves its positive as the nearest neighbor; values near zero indicate frequent mis-ranking of positives behind negatives. Together, alignment and uniformity decompose representation quality into ``similar things close'' and ``dissimilar things spread out''; MRR summarizes whether the metric space supports correct within-batch retrieval under the triplet mining setup.

\paragraph{Decoder-side metrics.}
Each aspect (MF, BP, CC) is evaluated under the shared UniRef50 cluster-aware split. We report mAP over terms with at least one positive in the split, and at the F1-optimal probability threshold (swept from 0.05 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05) macro-averaged F1, precision, recall, and MCC. The validation mAP-best model is evaluated on the test split.

\paragraph{Variant-level metrics.}
Where curated variant--GO effect labels exist, we evaluate agreement between predicted and reference LOSS / GAIN assignments at the term level (and report per-variant counts of each call type). DMS assay benchmarks can be analyzed separately using standard ranking metrics on assay scores rather than a derived scalar from GO probabilities.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Wild-type structure sources (AF2 vs.\ AF3)}
Before encoder training, we compared AFDB AF2, in-house AF3, and ColabFold AF2 wild-type models on the DMS audit cohort ($n{=}90$; Section~\ref{sec:wt-structure-concordance}). The audit showed that stable targets generally retain high graph overlap across predictors, whereas high-RMSD cases tend to be flexible across all three sources rather than uniquely affected by the AF2$\rightarrow$AF3 switch. We therefore treat the AFDB phase~0 / AF3 phase~1 split as a pragmatic design choice and report all encoder and decoder results under this setting.

\subsection{Encoder and decoder performance}
Decoder comparisons use five \emph{embedding sources} for the same \texttt{FFNDecoder} architecture and training recipe (UniRef50 cluster-aware splits; thresholded metrics at the F1-optimal probability threshold, swept 0.05--0.95):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ESM-2 baseline:} raw 650M ESM-2 protein embeddings (1{,}280-d), no ContVAR encoder.
    \item \textbf{Phase-0 GO pretraining:} GO-pretrained encoder only, without phase~1 DMS fine-tuning (256-d).
    \item \textbf{ESM-2 + Phase-0 GO pretraining:} concatenation of ESM-2 and phase-0-only encoder embeddings (1{,}536-d).
    \item \textbf{Phase-1 DMS specialization:} encoder after phase~0 and phase~1 (256-d graph embeddings).
    \item \textbf{ESM-2 + Phase-1 DMS specialization:} concatenation of ESM-2 and full ContVAR encoder embeddings (1{,}536-d).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Decoder discussion.}
Across MF, BP, and CC, wild-type GO prediction is dominated by ESM-2 sequence embeddings rather than graph-only representations. On test mAP, ESM-2 alone is best for MF (0.730) and CC (0.588); BP peaks with ESM-2\,+\,Phase-0 (0.449), with ESM-2 alone and ESM-2\,+\,ContVAR within one point. Concatenating ESM-2 with either phase-0-only or full ContVAR embeddings consistently improves over the corresponding 256-d graph encoder used in isolation, but does not uniformly beat raw ESM-2---suggesting that the decoder stage still relies heavily on pretrained sequence features while graph metric learning supplies an additional, aspect-dependent signal. ContVAR (full), despite strong phase~0 semantic organization and phase~1 DMS specialization in encoder space, yields the lowest decoder mAP on all three aspects (e.g.\ MF test 0.677 vs.\ 0.730 for ESM-2), which is consistent with DMS fine-tuning optimizing family-level variant discrimination rather than bulk GO term recovery on UniRef50-annotated wild types. Val--test gaps are small for every condition, indicating stable generalization under the cluster-aware split rather than a single incomplete ablation.

\paragraph{GO function-change discussion.}
The GO function-change cohort separates two related but substantially different capabilities. On the proteins represented in this cohort, the decoder recovers the wild-type GO background well: MF and CC reach high mAP across embedding sources, and BP improves sharply when GO-pretrained graph embeddings are used (Table~\ref{tab:wt-embedding-results}). However, this annotation-level competence does not transfer directly into reliable signed gain/loss prediction at the variant--GO-term level. The signed task asks whether a specific variant changes a specific molecular function, biological process, or cellular component relative to the wild type; this requires detecting a mutation-induced directional change rather than only recognizing that the wild-type protein is associated with the term.

Class imbalance is central to interpreting Table~\ref{tab:go-function-change-results}. The evaluated GO function-change labels are dominated by LOF records (8,169 LOF vs.\ 759 GOF), so accuracy and NPV are inflated whenever a model favors the majority loss class. For example, phase-0 GO pretraining gives high apparent MF accuracy (0.834) and high specificity/LOF recall (0.905), but its GOF recall is only 0.078 and its GOF F1 is 0.074. Similarly, MF and BP NPV values around 0.90 mainly reflect the large number of true LOF cases, not strong confidence for the minority GOF class. Therefore, the most informative single summary is MCC, which remains close to zero or negative for nearly all MF and BP settings. This indicates that the predictions capture little balanced association with the curated signed labels despite sometimes high accuracy.

The ontology-specific behavior is also informative. MF is the most strongly annotated wild-type aspect, but it is the weakest signed-change aspect: all MF MCC values are negative, and GOF precision remains below 0.08 across embedding sources. BP shows the most consistent, although still modest, positive association, with MCC around 0.04--0.06 for the ESM-2-containing models and the best GOF F1 near 0.18. CC differs from MF and BP because GOF precision is comparatively high (0.64--0.69) and GOF F1 can reach 0.55, but negative-class performance is poor (NPV around 0.36--0.38 and negative F1 around 0.43--0.50), so the overall MCC remains small. Thus, CC appears more capable of producing plausible gain calls for localization-related terms, while still failing to jointly recover both gain and loss directions in a balanced way.

Overall, these results suggest that ContVAR can learn and decode wild-type GO term structure, but the current inference rule and supervision are not sufficient for robust variant-level GOF/LOF discrimination. Phase~0 GO pretraining helps wild-type annotation and often improves LOF specificity, whereas phase~1 DMS specialization increases GOF recall in some settings without producing a consistent MCC gain. The limiting factor is therefore not only representation quality, but also the mismatch between protein-level GO decoder training and signed variant-effect supervision.


\section{Limitations}

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the present results. First, phase~1 DMS specialization is trained on a small number of protein families after filtering. The strict UniRef50-aware family-level split is important for avoiding leakage, but it leaves only two validation and two test families, making performance estimates sensitive to family composition and limiting the diversity of mutation-effect patterns available during training.

Second, the available variant-effect supervision does not directly match the final signed GO function-change task. Phase~1 learns from DMS-derived neutral/deleterious contrastive relationships, whereas the final inference problem asks whether a specific variant causes LOSS, GAIN, or NEUTRAL behavior for a specific GO term. The decoder is also trained primarily as a wild-type multi-label GO predictor. As a result, signed variant effects are inferred indirectly from changes in decoded GO probabilities rather than learned from a large supervised collection of variant--GO gain/loss labels.

Third, the curated GO function-change cohort is strongly imbalanced, with many more loss-of-function than gain-of-function records. As shown by MCC and minority-class recall, reliable GOF/LOF discrimination remains unresolved, especially for MF and BP.

Fourth, ContVAR depends on predicted monomer structures and precomputed ESM-2 residue features. This design makes the framework scalable, but it does not explicitly model protein complexes, ligand-bound conformations, membrane environments, post-translational modifications, or dynamic conformational states that may determine the functional effect of some variants. The use of AFDB and AF3 structures also assumes that wild-type structural models provide sufficient context for variant interpretation.

Finally, the current system should be interpreted as a research framework for term-resolved variant function prediction, not as a clinically validated diagnostic tool. Its predictions require external biological validation before use in clinical decision-making or therapeutic prioritization.


\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

\subsection{Conclusion}
This study introduced ContVAR, a two-stage graph representation learning framework for moving protein variant interpretation from scalar deleteriousness scoring toward term-resolved GO function-change assessment. The framework combines GO-semantic pretraining, DMS-based variant specialization, and per-aspect GO decoders so that wild-type and variant embeddings can be compared as LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL calls for individual GO terms.

The results show a clear separation between the easier annotation problem and the harder signed variant-effect problem. Phase~0 learns a stable ontology-aware embedding space, and the decoder recovers wild-type GO backgrounds well, especially when ESM-2 features are available. However, phase~1 DMS specialization generalizes weakly under the strict family-level split, and signed GOF/LOF prediction remains unreliable at the variant--GO-term level. ContVAR therefore establishes a reproducible framework and evaluation path, while showing that robust term-resolved variant interpretation will require stronger signed variant-effect supervision and broader family coverage.

\subsection{Future Work}
The most immediate direction is to expand the variant-effect supervision used in phase~1 while retaining protein-family-level separation during evaluation. Future versions should incorporate a larger set of DMS assays, clinically curated variant resources, and experimentally annotated GOF/LOF cases. In particular, phase~1 would benefit from more balanced coverage across enzymes, receptors, binding proteins, localization factors, and regulatory proteins, because signed function change is unlikely to be learned robustly from a narrow collection of assay types.

A second direction is to study the encoder training choices more systematically. Phase~0 currently uses fixed semantic-similarity thresholds and ontology sampling ratios, while phase~1 combines global triplet loss with a mutation-site local contrastive loss under streaming semi-hard negative mining. Future work should evaluate whether adaptive semantic thresholds, ontology-balanced sampling, or hard-negative mining for phase~0 improve transfer to held-out DMS families. These ablations would help determine whether the limiting factor is mainly data scale, the contrastive objective, or the way GO-pretrained geometry is specialized for mutation effects.

The local branch of the encoder could also be extended from a single mutated-residue embedding to a small mutation-centered residue neighborhood. This would allow the local contrastive objective to represent nearby spatial interactions around the substituted residue, while preserving the overall monomer-graph setting of the method.

A further direction is to replace the current feed-forward decoder with models that better match the structure of the prediction problem. In this work, the decoder receives a fixed protein embedding and predicts GO terms independently with a multi-label loss. This is a reasonable baseline, but it does not explicitly model dependencies among GO terms, parent--child consistency in the GO graph, or the paired relationship between wild-type and variant embeddings. More suitable decoder designs include hierarchy-aware GO decoders that propagate information over the GO directed acyclic graph, label-attention or transformer-style decoders that learn interactions between protein embeddings and GO-term embeddings, and Siamese or cross-attention decoders that jointly consume wild-type and variant representations. These models could be fine-tuned on variant--GO pairs with LOSS, GAIN, and NEUTRAL labels, using class-balanced or focal losses and term-specific threshold calibration to handle rare gain-of-function examples.

Finally, the encoder and decoder could be trained more tightly within a shared multi-task framework. Instead of treating GO semantic triplets, DMS fitness ranking, wild-type GO annotation, and signed function-change labels as mostly separate stages, future work could optimize these signals jointly or through staged fine-tuning with explicit regularization between objectives. This would test whether ContVAR can move from a reproducible proof-of-concept pipeline toward a more reliable term-resolved predictor of mutation-induced functional change.
