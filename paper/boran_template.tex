\author{}

\begin{abstract}
Determining the functional consequences of single amino acid variants (SAVs) remains difficult when models must link structure, mutational assays, and Gene Ontology (GO) loss- and gain-of-function statements rather than a single pathogenicity score. We present ContVAR, a two-stage pipeline: a residual GATv2 encoder on hybrid SALAD-style residue graphs (ESM-2 node features, sequence/spatial/stochastic neighbors) trained first on GO semantic triplets (phase~0; separate MF/BP/CC heads) and then on DMS protein-family triplets with joint global and mutation-site contrastive losses (phase~1); and per-aspect FFN decoders that map frozen embeddings to multi-label GO annotations and per-term variant function-change calls (LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL). Phase~0 graphs use AlphaFold Database models; phase~1 uses in-house AlphaFold3 structures. An audit on 90 DMS wild-types shows that $\sim$52\% fall in a concordant low-RMSD class with high deterministic graph overlap across AF2 and AF3 sources, supporting this mixed-source design for the stable majority. On held-out GO data, phase~0 encoders reach test MRR up to 0.62 (MF) and 0.61 (CC). After DMS fine-tuning (best epoch~76), the full encoder reports validation/test MRR $\approx$0.14 on exhaustive family triplets with low global and local contrastive loss. Wild-type GO decoder ablations over five embedding sources and all three GO aspects show that raw ESM-2 attains the highest test mAP on MF (0.73) and CC (0.59), that ESM-2 concatenated with phase-0 or full graph embeddings is competitive on every aspect and marginally strongest on BP ($\approx$0.45), and that the DMS-tuned graph encoder alone ranks last on each aspect---sequence and structure signals are complementary rather than redundant under UniRef50 cluster-aware splits.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The functional annotation of proteins and the accurate interpretation of single amino acid variants (SAVs) are essential for unraveling disease mechanisms, understanding oncogenic transformations, and guiding targeted therapeutics. Advances in high-throughput sequencing have produced large catalogs of coding variation; however, experimental characterization remains costly, leaving many variants classified as variants of uncertain significance (VUS).

Computational variant effect prediction (VEP) methods are therefore widely used. Classical approaches, such as SIFT~\cite{ng2001predicting}, PolyPhen-2~\cite{adzhubei2010method}, and CADD~\cite{kircher2014general}, rely on conservation and hand-crafted descriptors to estimate deleteriousness. More recent sequence-first models, including language-model-based VEP paradigms~\cite{meier2021language} and large-scale protein language models~\cite{lin2023evolutionary}, improve zero/few-shot generalization by learning distributional constraints from large corpora. However, most VEP pipelines still optimize scalar pathogenicity signals rather than structured function-level objectives.

In parallel, automated protein function prediction has moved from sequence-similarity transfer to deep neural annotation models such as DeepGOPlus~\cite{kulmanov2019deepgoplus}, DeepFRI~\cite{gligorijevic2021structure}, TALE~\cite{cao2021tale}, and network-aware graph models such as DeepGraphGO~\cite{zhang2021deepgraphgo}. These methods are effective for wild-type GO assignment, but they are generally not designed around anchor--positive--negative mutation triplets, and thus do not directly enforce mutation-sensitive metric geometry.

\paragraph{Problem statement.}
Current literature leaves a practical gap between (i) ontology-aware representation learning and (ii) mutation-specific discrimination at protein-family split level. In our setting, this gap appears as follows: GO similarity supervision exists only as semantic pairs/triplets on cached protein graphs, while DMS training requires dynamic hard-negative selection over many candidate variants per family. A model that ignores either side tends to underperform for one of two goals---semantic organization (GO) or mutation separation (DMS). We therefore target a joint training regime that preserves GO-informed structure in initialization and then sharpens mutation-aware separation with explicit local supervision at the altered residue.

This work introduces ContVAR, a graph metric-learning framework that explicitly connects GO-structured semantic priors with DMS-driven contrastive supervision. ContVAR builds residue graphs from monomer coordinates (e.g., AlphaFold2 models from the AlphaFold Database~\cite{jumper2021highly,varadi2022afdb}), combines ESM-2 embeddings with residue identity encodings, and trains a shared encoder with ontology-specific phase~0 heads followed by phase~1 DMS specialization under a joint global+local objective.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Background concepts}
Metric learning on biological objects often uses triplet-style objectives to impose relative distances under weak labels. In protein representation learning, this strategy is attractive when categorical labels are incomplete but pairwise or ranking signals are available (e.g., mutational fitness ordering, semantic similarity). Graph neural networks additionally permit explicit spatial topology and residue-level context, which are difficult to capture with sequence-only encoders.

\subsection{Recent approaches}

\paragraph{Variant effect prediction.}
Early VEP tools rely on evolutionary conservation and biophysical heuristics. SIFT~\cite{ng2001predicting} classifies substitutions as tolerated or damaging by comparing amino acid properties at conserved positions derived from homologous sequences. PolyPhen-2~\cite{adzhubei2010method} combines sequence-based features with structural information (solvent accessibility, dihedral angles) in a Naïve Bayes classifier. CADD~\cite{kircher2014general} moves beyond single-residue heuristics by integrating dozens of heterogeneous annotations (conservation, regulatory context, protein features) into a single pathogenicity score via a trained support vector machine.

The advent of large protein language models opened a sequence-first paradigm for VEP. ESM-1v~\cite{meier2021language} performs zero-shot prediction of the effect of missense mutations by scoring sequences with a masked-language-model objective, demonstrating that evolutionary co-variation implicit in pre-training data captures functional constraints without explicit labels. ESM-2~\cite{lin2023evolutionary} scales this to 650M--15B parameters and achieves strong performance on DMS benchmarks such as ProteinGym~\cite{notin2023proteingym}.

\paragraph{Protein function prediction.}
DeepGOPlus~\cite{kulmanov2019deepgoplus} combines per-residue CNN features over sequences with a sequence-similarity transfer step, reporting mAP gains over sequence-similarity alone across all three GO aspects. TALE~\cite{cao2021tale} uses a Transformer with a joint sequence-label embedding that ties GO term representation to sequence features, improving annotation on sparsely annotated functions. DeepFRI~\cite{gligorijevic2021structure} constructs residue contact graphs from PDB structures and applies graph convolutional networks, showing that structure-derived topology captures functional relationships missed by sequence alone. DeepGraphGO~\cite{zhang2021deepgraphgo} aggregates protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks with sequence features through a GNN, demonstrating large-scale multispecies GO prediction.

Despite this progress, the two families address adjacent but not identical problems: VEP models output variant-level disruption scores, while GO annotation models assign terms to wild-type sequences. Neither family explicitly enforces mutation-sensitive metric geometry that respects GO-level functional differences.

\subsection{Comparative discussion}
Compared with pure sequence models, structure-aware encoders can represent non-local residue couplings and interface geometry. Compared with direct GO classifiers, metric objectives are more flexible for ranking and retrieval-style analyses. ContVAR combines these two aspects by first injecting GO semantics in latent geometry (phase~0), then refining mutation separation in phase~1 with DMS triplets and local residue-level contrastive supervision.

\subsection{Research gap}
The missing component in many prior pipelines is a \emph{single training flow} that (a) starts from GO-semantic constraints, (b) operates on mutation-level triplets, and (c) explicitly supervises both whole-graph and mutation-site representations under memory-aware hard-negative mining~\cite{schroff2015facenet}. This gap motivates the ContVAR design.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Overall Framework}
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the ContVAR pipeline in three panels: the end-to-end data flow from structures to variant calls (A), the encoder layer stack with the supervision objectives of both training phases (B), and the per-aspect decoder used at variant inference time (C).

  \textbf{(B)}~Encoder layer stack (\texttt{DeepProteinGAT}): node features $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times1300}$ and edge attributes $E \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{E}|\times20}$ are first projected by the \texttt{edge\_encoder} ($\mathbb{R}^{20}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{128}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{128}$); two residual edge-conditioned GATv2 layers (4 heads, $h{=}128$, 512-d node states, LayerNorm + ELU) produce $\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{512}$ that branches into a global mean-pooled head and a mutation-site local head, each projecting to $\ell_2$-normalized $\mathbf{z}_g,\mathbf{z}_l \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$; phase~0 attaches three ontology heads (MF / BP / CC) trained with the semantic triplet loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{GO}}$ ($m_{go}{=}0.2$), and phase~1 jointly minimizes the global triplet $\mathcal{L}_g$ and local pairwise contrastive $\mathcal{L}_l$ under streaming semi-hard mining, with $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}=(\mathcal{L}_g+\mathcal{L}_l)/2$.
  \textbf{(C)}~Decoder and inference: \texttt{FFNDecoder} ($256\!\to\!512\!\to\!1024\!\to\!512\!\to\!|V_{\text{GO}}|$, BatchNorm / ReLU / Dropout) is trained per GO aspect with class-balanced multi-label BCE (\texttt{NULL\_FUNCTION} weighted by $\alpha{=}3$); at inference, $\sigma$-scored variant probabilities are joined with wild-type GO annotations from GOA, and per-term decisions ($\theta{=}0.1$) yield LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL labels per GO term.}

ContVAR is organized as two training stages that share a common protein identifier space:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mutation-aware graph encoder.} Training proceeds in two phases. \textbf{Phase~0} performs GO semantic-similarity triplet pretraining with ontology-specific heads (MF, BP, CC) on residue graphs. \textbf{Phase~1} specializes the same backbone on DMS protein-family triplets with a joint global graph-level and local mutation-site contrastive loss; streaming semi-hard negative mining is active from the first phase~1 epoch.
    \item \textbf{Supervised GO decoder and variant function-change inference.} A feed-forward decoder (\texttt{FFNDecoder}) is trained on frozen encoder embeddings as a multi-label GO classifier under UniRef50 cluster-aware splits~\cite{suzek2015uniref}, separately for each GO aspect (MF, BP, CC). At inference, variant embeddings are scored and combined with curated wild-type (WT) GO annotations from GOA to produce per-GO LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL calls.
\end{enumerate}
Encoder and decoder use different supervision signals---DMS-driven contrastive geometry versus protein-level GO multi-label annotations---and are linked through a persisted encoder checkpoint and protein embeddings keyed by UniProt identifier.

\subsection{Dataset Description}
\textbf{GO (phase~0) track.} Phase~0 consumes residue graphs and ontology-specific semantic-similarity tables under the shared UniRef50-aware protein split~\cite{suzek2015uniref}, which contains 122,190 proteins (97,771 train, 12,093 validation, 12,326 test). Positives use similarity $\geq 0.8$; negatives use similarity $\leq 0.2$ and are sampled from low/mid bins ([0.0,0.1), [0.1,0.2]) for hardness balancing. 

\textbf{DMS (phase~1) track.} The mutagenesis corpus is organized by protein family with one wild-type anchor, multiple positives, and multiple negatives per family. Starting from the ProteinGym Deep Mutational Scanning benchmark~\cite{notin2023proteingym}, we retained proteins with sequence length at most 656 residues, giving 97 unique proteins. When an assay annotated only a protein segment, the corresponding full-length sequence was reconstructed and variant coordinates were remapped to the full-length residue index so that the graph encoder always received complete protein context. For each protein, the positive pool was built from 50 neutral variants selected immediately above the assay cutoff indicating retained-functionality while the negative pool used the 50 lowest-fitness score variants as deleterious examples; selection was sequence-position stratified to reduce clustering in a single domain or local region. This produced exactly 9,700 curated variants before graph construction. The DMS split respects the UniRef50-aware protein split used for phase~0 and decoder training: if a DMS protein is present in that split, the wild type and all of its variants inherit the same train/validation/test assignment; DMS proteins absent from the UniRef50 split are conservatively assigned to training. The resulting fixed DMS split contains 97 families (93 train, 2 validation, 2 test), preventing variant-level leakage across held-out protein families. Raw structures are consumed as mmCIF/CIF files, and per-residue ESM-2 embeddings are loaded from a precomputed store keyed by normalized protein identifiers. Mutated residue positions are parsed from variant filenames during collation and mining.

\textbf{GO annotation / decoder track.} The supervised decoder uses the same UniRef50-aware protein universe and train/validation/test assignment as phase~0, but reformulates the data as protein-level multi-label GO prediction rather than semantic triplet learning. Curated UniProt-GOA Swiss-Prot annotations~\cite{uniprot2023,geneontology2021} provide Molecular Function (MF), Biological Process (BP), and Cellular Component (CC) labels, and one decoder is trained independently for each ontology. Thus, phase~0 and the decoder share the same protein split by identity, while differing in supervision format: phase~0 uses semantic-similarity triplets, whereas the decoder uses GO multi-hot label vectors.

\textbf{GO function-change cohort.} Because no directly usable dataset links protein variants to GO-level loss- or gain-of-function effects, we constructed a curated cohort from UniProt Swiss-Prot \texttt{variant} and \texttt{mutagenesis} feature descriptions. The cohort converts free-text experimental descriptions into atomic variant--GO effect records with a signed effect label (LOSS or GAIN). After description filtering, GO matching, manual rule-based cleanup, and project-specific sequence-length filtering, the final cohort used in ContVAR contains 6,159 proteins, 17,442 variants, and 18,126 variant--GO term matches.

\textbf{Structure interchange vs.\ cached graphs.} CIF/mmCIF is the portable format for atomic coordinates; serialized graph tensors cache parsing, featurization, and edge construction for reuse.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation / Preprocessing}
\textbf{Structure sources.} For the selected DMS wild-type and variant sequences, three-dimensional structures were generated with AlphaFold3~\cite{abramson2024alphafold3}. To reduce folding cost on the compute cluster, available assay/MSA resources were reused instead of regenerating alignments from scratch. For the larger GO protein set used in phase~0 and decoder embedding export, residue graphs were built from already available structures, primarily AlphaFold Database (AFDB) AlphaFold2 models~\cite{varadi2022afdb,jumper2021highly} when present and alternative structure resources otherwise. All structures are treated as fixed mmCIF/CIF inputs for graph construction. Because phase~0 and the decoder consume AFDB-derived graphs while phase~1 DMS training uses project AF3 predictions, we characterized DMS wild-type concordance between the two structure sources before training (Section~\ref{sec:wt-structure-concordance}).

\textbf{From structures to residue graphs.} Per-residue $\mathrm{C}_\alpha$ coordinates and amino-acid identities are extracted from monomer structures and assembled into PyTorch Geometric graphs~\cite{fey2019pyg}. Connectivity uses the hybrid edge scheme below; an optional pure $k$NN baseline (Graphein-style~\cite{ramirez2022graphein}) is available for ablations.

Node features concatenate a residue-identity one-hot and a per-residue ESM-2 embedding:
\[
\mathbf{x}_i = [\text{AAOneHot}_i \, \| \, \text{ESM2}_i] \in \mathbb{R}^{20+1280} = \mathbb{R}^{1300}.
\]
ESM-2 (650M-parameter variant~\cite{lin2023evolutionary}) is chosen as the per-residue feature source because it produces high-quality sequence-contextualized representations without requiring multiple sequence alignment (MSA) construction at inference time. The embeddings are pre-computed and frozen throughout encoder training, so the GATv2 encoder learns mutation-sensitive spatial geometry on top of fixed evolutionary-chemical context rather than attempting to learn both from scratch.

\textbf{Decoder label preparation.} After encoder training, protein-level graph embeddings are exported to H5 and matched to GOA annotations by UniProt identifier. For each ontology, annotations are converted into multi-hot vectors over GO terms observed in at least ten training proteins; a \texttt{NULL\_FUNCTION} output is appended to the vocabulary. Proteins without both an exported embedding and GOA annotation for the selected aspect are excluded from decoder training and evaluation.

\textbf{GO function-change cohort construction.} We first downloaded all Swiss-Prot \texttt{variant} and \texttt{mutagenesis} feature records. Since a single feature description can describe multiple functional consequences, descriptions were split into atomic statements. Descriptions matching deterministic syntactic patterns were split with rule-based rules; ambiguous multi-effect descriptions were split with an LLM. Statements describing only weak or uncertain effects were removed: descriptions containing qualitative modifiers such as ``slight'' or ``moderate'', or quantitative increase/decrease estimates below 50\%, were excluded. This filtering step retained 129,989 atomic descriptions.

Each atomic description was then assigned an effect category using rule-based lexical patterns. Loss-of-function descriptions were identified from terms such as ``abolish'' and ``impair'', gain-of-function descriptions from terms such as ``gain'' and ``enhance'', and location descriptions from cellular localization phrases such as ``nuclear entry'' or ``cytoplasmic redistribution''. Location was separated at this stage because GO matching used only MF and BP terms for ordinary loss/gain statements, but only CC terms for localization statements. Descriptions that represented effects not expressible as GO terms, such as generic protein stability changes, were discarded.

For GO term assignment, each remaining atomic description and all candidate GO terms were embedded with SciBERT~\cite{beltagy2019scibert}. Matching was performed in two passes. First, each variant description was matched against the wild-type protein's existing GO annotations, using the highest cosine-similarity GO term when the similarity was at least 0.87. Second, descriptions without an acceptable wild-type annotation match were searched against the full GO term pool with a stricter minimum similarity of 0.90. Although this full-pool fallback was initially intended for gain-of-function descriptions, it was also applied to loss-of-function descriptions because some experimentally described lost functions were absent from the wild-type annotation set. Descriptions still unmatched after embedding search were passed to an LLM together with the ten nearest GO terms by embedding similarity; the LLM selected a term only when one of the candidates expressed the described function, otherwise the record was discarded.

Localization records were finally converted into signed LOSS or GAIN labels according to directionality. For example, increased nuclear localization was treated as gain of the corresponding localization term, whereas decreased localization to the nucleus was treated as loss. We also corrected systematic embedding errors in which amino-acid or nucleic-acid specificity dominated the match incorrectly. Cases such as an alanine-related description matching a serine-specific GO term, or tRNA-related descriptions matching rRNA-specific terms, were detected rule-wise and replaced by the appropriate parent GO term (e.g.\ RNA acetylation rather than a mismatched RNA-species-specific child). Lastly, a small set of overly general GO terms that produced many high-similarity false matches, such as enzyme inhibitor activity, was identified during manual inspection and all of their matches were removed, eliminating approximately 1,000 records. For compatibility with SALAD~\cite{jendrusch2025salad} and downstream AlphaFold structure prediction, we retained only proteins with sequence length between 49 and 1,280 residues, yielding the final 6,159-protein, 17,442-variant, 18,126-match cohort.

\subsubsection{Hybrid residue-graph connectivity}
\label{sec:hybrid-graph}
The same hybrid residue-graph construction is used for protein graphs across the encoder workflow, including GO semantic pretraining and DMS specialization. The design is motivated by the need to represent both local structural neighborhoods and non-local residue coupling: in the DMS setting, for example, variants can perturb local side-chain packing as well as distant contacts. A purely sequence-local chain graph under-represents 3D contacts; a fixed-radius spatial graph can miss compensatory long-range interactions; and a dense residue--residue graph is prohibitive for long chains. ContVAR therefore uses a \emph{fixed neighbor budget per residue} that mixes three complementary link types.

Let $N$ be the number of residues in a chain, $\mathbf{r}_i\in\mathbb{R}^3$ the $\mathrm{C}_\alpha$ coordinate of residue $i$, and $s_i$ its sequence index. Pairwise Euclidean distances $d_{ij}=\|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j\|_2$ and index distances $|s_i-s_j|$ are computed within the same chain (cross-chain links are masked). For each residue $i$, neighbors are selected in three sequential stages; edges already chosen in an earlier stage are excluded from later stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sequence-index neighbors} ($k_{\text{idx}}$, default 16): the $k_{\text{idx}}$ residues $j\neq i$ on the same chain with smallest $|s_i-s_j|$.
    \item \textbf{Spatial neighbors} ($k_{\text{sp}}$, default 16): among remaining candidates, the $k_{\text{sp}}$ residues with smallest $d_{ij}$.
    \item \textbf{Stochastic long-range neighbors} ($k_{\text{rnd}}$, default 16): among remaining candidates, sample $k_{\text{rnd}}$ partners via a Gumbel top-$k$ trick~\cite{kool2019stochastic}. Unnormalized scores are $w_{ij}=-3\log(d_{ij}+\epsilon)$; independent Gumbel noise is added and the lowest perturbed scores are taken, biasing selection toward closer residues while retaining diversity (inverse-cube weighting in distance space).
\end{enumerate}
Directed edges $(i\to j)$ are created for every valid neighbor slot; invalid padding slots ($j=-1$) are dropped. Each edge carries a 20-dimensional attribute vector: (i) a 16-bin Gaussian radial basis expansion of $d_{ij}$ on $[0,d_{\max}]$ with $d_{\max}=22$\,\AA; (ii) a 3-way one-hot encoding of neighbor type (index / spatial / random); and (iii) one normalized sequence separation $|s_i-s_j|/N$. The GATv2 stack consumes these attributes through an edge MLP before attention (Section~\ref{sec:encoder-backbone}).

This recipe is motivated by sparse-neighbor designs for structure modeling that combine chain-index, Euclidean nearest, and randomized long-range partners~\cite{jendrusch2025salad}; ContVAR adapts the connectivity pattern to residue-level message passing on AlphaFold2-predicted monomers~\cite{jumper2021highly}, with $k{=}16$ per neighbor type by default and on-disk graph caching when structures are revisited.

\textbf{Mutation context.} The encoder always sees the full-chain graph; locality around the variant is enforced in the loss via the mutation-site branch rather than by cropping the graph to a fixed-radius neighborhood.

\subsubsection{Structure-source concordance (AF3 vs.\ AFDB AF2)}
\label{sec:wt-structure-concordance}
Phase~0 and decoder training rely primarily on AFDB monomer models (AF2), whereas phase~1 DMS triplets use in-house AF3 wild-type and variant predictions. Before committing to this split, we audited whether mixed sources would systematically change the \emph{fixed} residue graphs fed to the encoder, or whether disagreement is dominated by intrinsically flexible targets irrespective of the predictor. This is a dataset-quality check, not a model-evaluation result.

\paragraph{Global C$\alpha$ RMSD.}
Table~\ref{tab:wt-graph-summary} summarizes pairwise C$\alpha$ RMSD after superposition for $n{=}90$ DMS wild-types with in-house AF3, AFDB AF2, and ColabFold AF2 rank-1 structures~\cite{mirdita2022colabfold}. Seven families are excluded because no AFDB model was available (P03466, P03470, P03704, P04610, P04618, P16117, P03034). Three comparisons are reported: AFDB vs.\ project AF3 (phase~0 vs.\ phase~1 source), AFDB vs.\ ColabFold AF2, and project AF3 vs.\ ColabFold AF2. Cohort medians are \textbf{6.0}, \textbf{5.0}, and \textbf{6.1~\AA}, respectively (means 9.9, 9.5, and 10.1~\AA). Compact folds remain at the low end (e.g.\ TPK1 Q9H3S4, 0.17~\AA\ across all three pairs; GFP P42212, 3.0~\AA\ AFDB--AF3). The long tail is shared across comparisons (SPG1 P06654: 63.0, 65.0, and 57.8~\AA), indicating that elevated displacement on flexible targets is not specific to the AF2$\rightarrow$AF3 switch.

\paragraph{Cross-pipeline agreement at 7~\AA\ cutoff.}
To classify targets by structural stability across all three structure sources, we binarized each comparison at 7~\AA\ RMSD (Table~\ref{tab:wt-rmsd-categories}). \textbf{47} of 90 audited wild-types ($\sim$52\%) are low in all three pairwise comparisons; \textbf{34} are high in all three; only \textbf{9} are mixed (five with one high and two low; four with two high and one low). The \emph{all three low} set shows concordant, low-displacement folds with high deterministic graph overlap (Section~\ref{sec:wt-graph-overlap}), i.e.\ different predictors agree on both global coordinates and the fixed SALAD edge sets used in training. Most mixed-category proteins sit just above the 7~\AA\ threshold in one comparison while the other two remain low, so they behave like the stable cohort in practice. Only two identifiers---P63165 and P51681---show project AF3 clearly diverging from \emph{both} AFDB and ColabFold AF2; P51681 is also among the 26 targets with deterministic Jaccard $<0.8$. Proteins high across all three comparisons overlap strongly with the flexible long tail above (e.g.\ SPG1 P06654) and with low deterministic graph Jaccard, indicating that large displacements reflect intrinsic conformational variability rather than a systematic AF2$\rightarrow$AF3 pipeline effect.

\paragraph{Within-predictor variability (spot checks).}
Representative high-RMSD targets were additionally re-folded with AlphaFold Server (AF3) and ColabFold (custom MSA). On SPG1 (P06654), fresh AF3 and AFDB AF2 still differ by $\sim$56--66~\AA; within a single predictor, rank-1 vs.\ rank-2 models differ by $\sim$19~\AA\ (AlphaFold Server) and $\sim$33~\AA\ (ColabFold custom MSA). Low-RMSD proteins stay low across pipelines. ColabFold rank-1 vs.\ AFDB AF2 in the same audit therefore captures one AF2 reference point.

\paragraph{Residue-graph overlap.}
\label{sec:wt-graph-overlap}
Structures alone do not determine encoder inputs: graphs use the hybrid SALAD connectivity above ($k{=}16$ per neighbor type). In a separate pass, we rebuilt graphs from AF3 and AFDB wild-type CIFs with the same code path as training (deterministic index/spatial neighbors; random neighbors with fixed seed 42) and compared edge sets by Jaccard overlap on residue-indexed pairs, together with Pearson correlation of per-residue degrees on shared indices ($n{=}90$, matching the RMSD audit cohort).

Across these 90 pairs, median Jaccard was \textbf{0.92} (all edges), \textbf{0.84} (spatial only), and \textbf{1.00} (sequence-index only); median degree-profile correlation was \textbf{0.96}. Global C$\alpha$ RMSD and graph overlap measure different aspects: many families show modest displacement in all three RMSD pairs while still sharing most SALAD edges, whereas the flexible tail is high in both metrics.

Using deterministic overlap (index + spatial, excluding stochastic long-range links), 26 targets fell below 0.8 (Table~\ref{tab:wt-graph-summary}). On these 26 proteins, median AFDB--AF3 RMSD is \textbf{22.9~\AA}\ versus \textbf{2.8~\AA}\ for the other 64 ($n{=}90$); AFDB--ColabFold AF2 and AF3--ColabFold AF2 medians show the same split (18.9 and 20.9~\AA\ vs.\ 2.6 and 3.1~\AA). At the 7~\AA\ cutoff, 20 of 26 exceed the threshold in all three RMSD pairs, so low graph overlap and high structural displacement largely co-occur on the same flexible wild-types (e.g.\ YAP1, p53, CD19, SERT, $\alpha$-synuclein). Five audit-stable examples (P69222, P31969, O15245, P80324, P61991) remain below 7~\AA\ in all three comparisons (3.8--6.8~\AA\ AFDB--AF3) yet still show deterministic Jaccard $0.57$--$0.75$---modest graph agreement despite concordant global folds. For the remaining 64 families with deterministic Jaccard $\geq 0.8$, AF2/AF3 graph inputs are closely aligned.

\paragraph{Implications for the AFDB/AF3 split.}
The audit supports using AFDB AlphaFold2 graphs for phase~0 GO pretraining and project AF3 structures for phase~1 DMS triplets without harmonizing every target to a single predictor. The GO pretraining corpus is dominated by well-defined, globular proteins that fall into the concordant \emph{all three low} regime; for this majority, AFDB coordinates and SALAD graphs are stable across AF2 and AF3 sources, so phase~0 is unlikely to inherit a strong structure-predictor bias. On the flexible \emph{all three high} tail, large RMSD values appear consistently across AFDB, ColabFold, and in-house AF3, so disagreement is better explained by target-intrinsic disorder or multi-state behavior than by the choice to mix predictors across training phases. The nine mixed-category proteins do not define a separate failure mode for the pipeline: aside from P63165 and P51681, they are threshold-borderline cases aligned with the stable cohort. We therefore retain the phase~0 (AFDB) / phase~1 (AF3) structure split and the fixed hybrid edge construction described in Section~\ref{sec:hybrid-graph}.

\subsection{Model/Architecture Design}

\subsubsection{Mutation-Aware Graph Encoder (backbone)}
\label{sec:encoder-backbone}
The encoder is a residual two-layer GATv2 stack~\cite{brody2022how} with four attention heads per layer and hidden size 128. GATv2 is selected over standard GAT~\cite{velickovic2018gat} because the original GAT computes attention coefficients as a function of a linear combination of the concatenated source and target features, which Brody et al.~\cite{brody2022how} show collapses to a ranking over neighbors that is independent of the query node—making attention static. GATv2 remedies this by applying the non-linearity \emph{before} the attention projection so that each coefficient depends jointly on both the query and the key, yielding fully dynamic attention. In the context of irregular protein residue graphs, where the chemical environment around a mutation site varies substantially across different protein families and positions, this dynamic re-weighting is important: the encoder must learn to attend to non-local compensatory contacts whose relevance changes depending on which residue is mutated. Edge attributes are first projected by an MLP and injected into both GATv2 layers. Let $\mathbf{h}_i^{(l)}$ be node state at layer $l$; each layer applies attention-weighted aggregation with edge conditioning, layer normalization, and residual addition. Global embedding uses mean pooling followed by a projection head to 256 dimensions and $\ell_2$ normalization. A separate local projection head extracts the embedding at mutation position; if a residue index is absent, graph mean is used as fallback.

\subsubsection{Phase 0: GO semantic pretraining}
Phase~0 provides biologically informed initialization by training ontology-specific triplets (MF/BP/CC) using ontology heads on top of the shared encoder. For each ontology $o$:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GO},o}=\max(0,\ d(\mathbf{z}_a^o,\mathbf{z}_p^o)-d(\mathbf{z}_a^o,\mathbf{z}_n^o)+m_{go}),
\]
with default $m_{go}=0.2$. Active ontology losses are averaged per step. Optional ratio-based ontology sampling (default MF:BP:CC = 0.5:0.3:0.2) prevents dominance by a single ontology.

\subsubsection{Phase 1: DMS encoder training (global triplet + local contrastive loss)}
\label{sec:phase1-dms}
After phase~0, phase~1 loads DMS protein-family triplets under the UniRef50-respecting fixed split described above (97 families: 93 train, 2 validation, 2 test) and initializes weights from the phase~0 checkpoint. Phase~1 uses streaming semi-hard negative mining~\cite{schroff2015facenet} from the first training epoch: each epoch reshuffles protein families, samples positives, scans candidate negatives in chunks, and rebuilds mined batches online. Exhaustive positive$\times$negative enumeration is reserved for validation and test.

\paragraph{Per-batch mining and forward pass.}
Families are shuffled and grouped into mining batches. Gradients are accumulated over four mined batches before each optimizer update. For each batch:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item One positive variant per family is sampled; anchor and positive residue graphs are loaded from cache or built on the fly.
    \item The current model embeds anchors and positives (no gradient) to obtain $d_{ap}=d(\mathbf{z}_a^g,\mathbf{z}_p^g)$.
    \item All candidate negatives listed for that family are scanned in chunks of 10 structures; negatives that are hard ($d_{an}<d_{ap}$) or semi-hard ($d_{ap}\le d_{an}<d_{ap}+m$) are retained and up to 10 per family are subsampled at random; if none qualify, the closest negative is used.
    \item A gradient step runs on the mined set; the global triplet hinge $\max(0, d_{ap}-d_{an}+m)$ is averaged over all qualifying negatives in the batch.
\end{enumerate}
The global loss is therefore always computed on mined hard/semi-hard negatives, not on a fixed random subset chosen once per epoch.

\paragraph{Losses.}
For mined negatives, the global branch is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{g}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{n\in\mathcal{Q}}\max\bigl(0,\ d(\mathbf{z}_a^g,\mathbf{z}_p^g)-d(\mathbf{z}_a^g,\mathbf{z}_n^g)+m\bigr),
\]
where $\mathcal{Q}$ is the set of hard and semi-hard negatives retained for the batch. The local branch supervises mutation-site embeddings. Global mean pooling can dilute mutation-specific signal in long proteins; the local head extracts the embedding at the parsed mutation index and applies a pairwise contrastive loss that attracts anchor--positive pairs and repels anchor--negative pairs at the mutation site:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{l}=\frac{1}{2B}\sum_{i=1}^{2B}\left[y_i d_i^2 + (1-y_i)\max(0,m-d_i)^2\right],
\]
with $y_i=1$ on positive pairs and $y_i=0$ on negative pairs. The hardest global negative per family (index from mining stats) is reused to select which mutation-site negative enters $\mathcal{L}_l$. The step objective is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}=\frac{\mathcal{L}_{g}+\mathcal{L}_{l}}{2}.
\]

\paragraph{Optimization, validation, and checkpoints.}
Phase~1 training runs for 200 epochs by default. Optimizer: AdamW (learning rate $10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01) with gradient accumulation over four steps and gradient clipping at norm 1.0. Validation and test enumerate every positive$\times$negative pair per family for stable ranking metrics. The phase~1 checkpoint with lowest validation $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$ is retained for embedding export and decoder training.

\subsubsection{Supervised GO decoder}
\label{sec:decoder}

\paragraph{Scope.}
The decoder consumes protein-level embeddings from the trained encoder (256 dimensions) or, in ablations, raw ESM-2 embeddings (1{,}280 dimensions) or their concatenation (1{,}536 dimensions). It outputs multi-label GO probabilities under Molecular Function (MF), Biological Process (BP), and Cellular Component (CC). One decoder is trained independently per aspect.

\paragraph{GO annotation source and vocabulary.}
Labels are taken from UniProt-GOA Swiss-Prot~\cite{uniprot2023,geneontology2021}, excluding electronic (\texttt{IEA}) and ``no data'' (\texttt{ND}) evidence codes. The vocabulary retains GO terms annotated in at least ten training proteins. A dedicated \texttt{NULL\_FUNCTION} class is appended to flag predicted complete loss of function.

\paragraph{Dataset and UniRef50-aware splits.}
Training proteins are partitioned by UniRef50 cluster so that no cluster appears in more than one of train, validation, or test. This same protein-level partition is used by phase~0 and by all three decoder aspects, and the DMS family split inherits it where the DMS protein is present in the UniRef50 mapping. Each protein is represented by a multi-hot label vector over the aspect vocabulary.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
\texttt{FFNDecoder} is a feed-forward network. For the 256-d and 1{,}280-d inputs the hidden widths are $512 \to 1024 \to 512$; for the 1{,}536-d concatenated input they are $1024 \to 512$. Each hidden block applies batch normalization, ReLU activation, and dropout ($p=0.3$, tapered to $p \approx 0.2$ in the last hidden block). The head outputs logits per GO term; sigmoid is applied at inference.

\paragraph{Class-balanced multi-label loss.}
Training uses binary cross-entropy with logits and positive-class weights. For GO term $c$,
\[
w_c \;=\; \min\!\left(\frac{N - n_c^{+}}{n_c^{+}+\varepsilon},\ \tau\right),\qquad
w_{\text{NULL\_FUNCTION}} \;\leftarrow\; \alpha \cdot w_{\text{NULL\_FUNCTION}},
\]
where $n_c^{+}$ is the number of training proteins with term $c$, $N$ is the training set size, $\tau=20$, and $\alpha=3.0$.

\paragraph{Optimization and schedule.}
AdamW with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-4}$; cosine annealing over 100 epochs; batch size 256. The best validation mAP checkpoint is kept; training stops if mAP fails to improve by more than $10^{-4}$ for ten consecutive epochs. Metrics are logged to Weights \& Biases.

\paragraph{Decoder evaluation metrics.}
Validation and test use logits converted to probabilities. GO terms with zero positives in a split are excluded from mAP. For thresholded metrics we sweep a probability grid from 0.05 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05 and select the threshold maximizing macro F1; at the selected threshold we report macro-averaged F1, precision, recall, subset accuracy, and MCC.

\paragraph{Variant function-change inference.}
The decoder is run on \emph{variant} embeddings only; wild-type GO annotations are looked up directly from GOA (the database is the source of truth for the WT). For a variant with embedding $\mathbf{z}_{\text{var}}$ let $\mathbf{s}=\sigma(\text{FFNDecoder}(\mathbf{z}_{\text{var}}))\in[0,1]^C$. For each annotated WT GO term $g$:
\[
\text{status}(g) =
\begin{cases}
\text{LOSS} & \text{if } g \in \mathrm{WT}_{\text{GO}} \text{ and } s_g < 1-\theta,\\
\text{GAIN} & \text{if } g \notin \mathrm{WT}_{\text{GO}} \text{ and } s_g > \theta,\\
\text{NEUTRAL} & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\]
with default $\theta=0.1$. Variant outputs are therefore \emph{term-resolved} function-change calls rather than a single pooled score over all wild-type annotations.

\paragraph{Batch variant scoring.}
Variant embeddings are scored in batch; wild-type accessions and mutation tokens are parsed from record identifiers. Variants whose wild type lacks GOA coverage are skipped. Predicted LOSS / GAIN / NEUTRAL calls can be compared against curated variant--GO effect labels (e.g.\ the Swiss-Prot function-change cohort) or summarized as per-variant counts of each call type.

\subsubsection{Interactive Hugging Face Space}
\label{sec:hf-space}
To make ContVAR accessible beyond offline batch execution, we also provide an interactive Hugging Face Space for structure-level inference. The Space exposes two user workflows. In the single-protein workflow, users upload one PDB or CIF structure and receive ranked GO predictions across MF, BP, and CC. In the WT-versus-variant workflow, users upload matched wild-type and variant structures and receive WT score, variant score, score difference, and a derived gained/lost/stable status for the highest-changing GO terms. Results are displayed as aspect-specific tables and summary panels, with downloadable prediction files for downstream inspection.

The Space runs the same graph-based inference path used by the project: it parses uploaded structures, extracts the amino-acid sequence and residue coordinates, computes ESM-2 residue embeddings on the fly, constructs the hybrid residue graph, applies the trained ContVAR encoder, and decodes GO scores with the per-aspect FFN decoders. The public interface is intended as a lightweight demonstration and inspection layer rather than a replacement for large-scale batch scoring.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\paragraph{Encoder-side metrics.}
During phase~0 and phase~1 we log total, global, and local losses; global and local distance margins; streaming-mining statistics for hard, semi-hard, easy, evaluated, and qualifying candidate negatives; and three geometry/retrieval diagnostics on $\ell_2$-normalized global embeddings~\cite{wang2020hypersphere}. Validation and test use exhaustive positive$\times$negative combinations per family. The encoder checkpoint with lowest validation total loss is re-evaluated on held-out families before decoder training.

\textbf{Alignment} measures how close anchor and positive embeddings are. Following Wang and Isola~\cite{wang2020hypersphere}, we report the mean squared Euclidean distance between paired embeddings,
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\|\mathbf{z}_a^{(i)}-\mathbf{z}_p^{(i)}\|_2^2,
\]
where $B$ is the batch size. Lower values indicate that functionally similar variants (anchors and their positives) occupy nearby points on the unit hypersphere---the ``alignment'' objective of contrastive learning.

\textbf{Uniformity} quantifies how evenly embeddings fill the hypersphere rather than collapsing into a narrow cone. On the concatenated set of anchor, positive, and negative embeddings $\{\mathbf{z}\}$, we compute
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{unif}} \;=\; \log\!\left(\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{(\mathbf{z}_i,\mathbf{z}_j)\in\mathcal{P}} e^{-t\,\|\mathbf{z}_i-\mathbf{z}_j\|_2^2}\right),
\]
with temperature $t{=}2$ and $\mathcal{P}$ the set of unordered pairs ($|\mathcal{P}|$ from pairwise distances). Lower (more negative) values indicate greater spread. This matches the reference implementation (\url{https://github.com/ssnl/align_uniform}) of Wang and Isola~\cite{wang2020hypersphere}.

\textbf{Mean reciprocal rank (MRR)} evaluates retrieval within each mini-batch. For anchor $i$, all positives and negatives in the batch form a candidate pool; candidates are ranked by ascending $\ell_2$ distance to the anchor. The rank $r_i$ of the matching positive (the $i$-th positive) yields $\mathrm{MRR}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_i 1/r_i$. MRR${=}1$ when every anchor retrieves its positive as the nearest neighbor; values near zero indicate frequent mis-ranking of positives behind negatives. Together, alignment and uniformity decompose representation quality into ``similar things close'' and ``dissimilar things spread out''; MRR summarizes whether the metric space supports correct within-batch retrieval under the triplet mining setup.

\paragraph{Decoder-side metrics.}
Each aspect (MF, BP, CC) is evaluated under the shared UniRef50 cluster-aware split. We report mAP over terms with at least one positive in the split, and at the F1-optimal probability threshold (swept from 0.05 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05) macro-averaged F1, precision, recall, and MCC. The validation mAP-best model is evaluated on the test split.

\paragraph{Variant-level metrics.}
Where curated variant--GO effect labels exist, we evaluate agreement between predicted and reference LOSS / GAIN assignments at the term level (and report per-variant counts of each call type). DMS assay benchmarks can be analyzed separately using standard ranking metrics on assay scores rather than a derived scalar from GO probabilities.

\paragraph{Decoder discussion.}
Across MF, BP, and CC, wild-type GO prediction is dominated by ESM-2 sequence embeddings rather than graph-only representations. On test mAP, ESM-2 alone is best for MF (0.730) and CC (0.588); BP peaks with ESM-2\,+\,Phase-0 (0.449), with ESM-2 alone and ESM-2\,+\,ContVAR within one point. Concatenating ESM-2 with either phase-0-only or full ContVAR embeddings consistently improves over the corresponding 256-d graph encoder used in isolation, but does not uniformly beat raw ESM-2---suggesting that the decoder stage still relies heavily on pretrained sequence features while graph metric learning supplies an additional, aspect-dependent signal. ContVAR (full), despite strong phase~0 semantic organization and phase~1 DMS specialization in encoder space, yields the lowest decoder mAP on all three aspects (e.g.\ MF test 0.677 vs.\ 0.730 for ESM-2), which is consistent with DMS fine-tuning optimizing family-level variant discrimination rather than bulk GO term recovery on UniRef50-annotated wild types. Val--test gaps are small for every condition, indicating stable generalization under the cluster-aware split rather than a single incomplete ablation.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

\subsection{Conclusion}
ContVAR connects GO-structured metric learning on residue graphs with ontology-level decoding for variant interpretation. The encoder is pretrained on semantic GO triplets (phase~0) and specialized on DMS anchor--positive--negative families (phase~1) using a hybrid graph built from monomer coordinates and ESM-2 features; three per-aspect FFN decoders then predict wild-type GO annotations and, at inference, per-term LOSS/GAIN/NEUTRAL function-change calls~\cite{schroff2015facenet,suzek2015uniref}.

Our experiments support this design as an end-to-end benchmark rather than a single embedding winner on every task. A wild-type structure audit (Section~\ref{sec:wt-structure-concordance}) shows that most DMS families have concordant AF2/AF3 coordinates and SALAD graphs, justifying AFDB graphs in phase~0 and project AF3 graphs in phase~1. Phase~0 encoders organize GO triplets effectively (test MRR up to 0.62 on MF and 0.61 on CC; Table~\ref{tab:encoder-phase0}). Phase~1 further compresses DMS discrimination at the cost of lower batch MRR ($\approx$0.13--0.14; Table~\ref{tab:encoder-phase1}), reflecting the harder family-level retrieval objective. On wild-type GO prediction (Table~\ref{tab:decoder-ablation}), test mAP is highest with ESM-2 on MF (0.73) and CC (0.59), with ESM-2 concatenated to graph embeddings marginally strongest on BP ($\approx$0.45) and competitive elsewhere. The DMS-tuned ContVAR (full) embedding ranks below every other source on all three aspects. Graph metric learning therefore adds measurable signal when fused with ESM-2, but does not supplant sequence priors for bulk GO annotation under the present decoder recipe.

Taken together, ContVAR provides a reproducible two-phase training stack, explicit structure-source justification, and a multi-embedding decoder comparison that separates GO semantic pretraining gains from DMS specialization and from raw ESM-2 baselines. The present study reports encoder geometry metrics and wild-type GO decoder performance under validation mAP-best checkpoints across five embedding sources.
